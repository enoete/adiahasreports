Math also reflects this: a high T1 (91%) dipping in T2 (80%) when fractions and shape geometry introduced more written reasoning.

Overall Analysis — The Inflated Baseline Year

Grade 4 scores are the highest Adiaha has recorded across her academic career — ELA 80%, Math 83%, Social Studies 83%, Science 85%, Spanish 86%. But these numbers must be read alongside the do-over policy that was in place this year, which allowed students to redo assessments and add marks to their totals. This artificially elevated the scores, creating a baseline that Grade 5 (operating under standard conditions) was always going to look worse against.

What the teacher narratives confirm is that the underlying patterns were identical to every other year. Ms. Oehlert's Math comments note verbatim: "Adiaha tends to make small mistakes in her work due to working too fast or being distracted. When she slows down and takes her time, Adiaha demonstrates grade appropriate skills." This is the same observation made by Ms. Mickle in Grade 2, Mrs. Dolphin in Grade 3, and Ms. Mahon in Grade 5. The scores changed; Adiaha didn't.

The most clinically valuable data point in this report card is buried in the ELA narrative: Adiaha reads 156 words per minute (79th percentile) but scores at the 59th percentile for comprehension. This fluency-comprehension gap — strong decoding with lagging retention — is a well-documented signature of dyslexic-adjacent reading profiles. She can read fast; holding and processing what she reads under pressure is harder. This is worth sharing with any specialist.

The behavior narrative from Ms. Oehlert includes a line that deserves attention: "This is not due to a lack of effort or respect — Adiaha genuinely gets distracted and feels bad when this happens." That self-awareness is important. It rules out defiance and confirms what four years of reports suggest: an attention regulation pattern that is neurological, not motivational.

Teacher: Terry-Ann Mahon  ·  St. Kitts International Academy
Moving to Grade 6

Overall Analysis — Reading This Report in Context

Grade 5 represents the most demanding academic year Adiaha has faced, and the scores reflect exactly that — but they tell a story of recovery and growth, not failure. Several important factors shape how this report should be read:

Grade 4 was inflated. The Grade 4 system allowed do-overs to be added to grades, artificially lifting scores. That context makes the Grade 5 numbers look like a larger drop than they truly are. The real comparison point is Grade 3, and in that light, Adiaha is navigating a harder curriculum with reasonable footing.

The T1 → T2 trajectory is the real headline. She improved in every graded subject from Term 1 to Term 2: ELA (+6), Science (+14), Spanish (+3), Math (+5), and Social Studies was the only subject that moved in the other direction (-6), likely reflecting the heavier essay-and-map-skills demands of the Asia unit. This mid-year recovery is even more remarkable when you consider that Adiaha began the year with undiagnosed visual difficulties — she did not receive her glasses until approximately two months into Term 1. That means her lowest scores were recorded while she was literally struggling to see the board. The improvement from Term 1 to Term 2 reflects not just academic adjustment, but a student finally learning under the conditions she should have had from day one. That is a genuine marker of resilience and determination.

Math at 63% is the one area that needs deliberate support in Grade 6. The teacher is right: she understands the concepts orally but does not write her working clearly — a pattern consistent with dyslexic processing of symbolic notation. Multiplication table fluency and "show your steps" habits are the two practical targets heading into Grade 6.

Science at 79% looks like a dip from her historical highs, but the T1→T2 jump from 72 to 86 is extraordinary — the highest single-subject term improvement on the entire report card. She remains a natural scientist. The Teacher's comment calling her a "budding scientist" who "confidently contributes and makes useful links between course content and her own experiences" is the Adiaha we know.
